1.1 Real-world Examples of Linear Systems

1.1 Real-world Examples of Linear Systems#

The aim of this course is to study systems of linear differential or difference equations, such as arise in the analysis of many situations in the sciences.

In this section we shall introduce these concepts via a number of examples.


Spring-mass system

Example 1.1.1 Assuming that damping effects are negligible, then for a vibrating system as shown the displacements \(x_1, x_2\) of the masses \(m_1, m_2\) from the equilibrium position are given by the system of equations

\[\begin{split} m_1\frac{\mathrm{d}^2x_1}{\mathrm{d}t^2} &= -k_1x_1-k_3(x_1-x_2),\\ m_2\frac{\mathrm{d}^2x_2}{\mathrm{d}t^2} &= -k_2x_2-k_3(x_2-x_1). \end{split}\]

That is

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}^2}{\mathrm{d}t^2}\left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{cc}-\frac{k_1+k_3}{m_1} & \frac{k_3}{m_1}\\ \frac{k_3}{m_2}& -\frac{k_2+k_3}{m_2}\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\end{array} \right), \end{split}\]

or

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}^2\mathbf{x}}{\mathrm{d}t^2} = A\mathbf{x}, \]

where

\[\begin{split} \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\end{pmatrix} \text{ and } A = \left(\begin{array}{cc}-\frac{k_1+k_3}{m_1} & \frac{k_3}{m_1}\\ \frac{k_3}{m_2}& -\frac{k_2+k_3}{m_2}\end{array}\right). \end{split}\]

Alternatively, the above system of second order differential equations can be replaced by one containing first order ones by writing \(\displaystyle\frac{\mathrm{d}x_1}{\mathrm{d}t}=v_1\) and \(\displaystyle\frac{\mathrm{d}x_2}{\mathrm{d}t}=v_2\), so that

\[\begin{split} m_1\frac{\mathrm{d}v_1}{\mathrm{d}t} &= -k_1x_1-k_3(x_1-x_2),\\ m_2\frac{\mathrm{d}v_2}{\mathrm{d}t} &= -k_2x_2-k_3(x_2-x_1). \end{split}\]

Then

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\\v_1\\v_2\end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{cccc}0&0&1&0\\0&0&0&1\\-\frac{k_1+k_3}{m_1}&\frac{k_3}{m_1} &0&0\\\frac{k_3}{m_2}&-\frac{k_2+k_3}{m_2}&0&0\end{array}\right) \begin{pmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

Note

It is common practice to replace a single \(n\)th order differential equation of the form

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}^nx}{\mathrm{d}t^n}+a_1\frac{\mathrm{d}^{n-1}x}{\mathrm{d}t^{n-1}}+a_2\frac{\mathrm{d}^{n-2}x}{\mathrm{d}t^{n-2}} +\cdots +a_{n-1}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}+a_nx = 0, \]

by a system of first order differential equations obtained by writing

\[ \displaystyle x_1=x, x_2=\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}, x_3=\frac{\mathrm{d}^2x}{\mathrm{d}t^2},\ldots, x_{n}=\frac{\mathrm{d}^{n-1}x}{\mathrm{d}t^{n-1}}, \]

so that

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}x_{n}}{\mathrm{d}t}+a_1x_{n}+a_2x_{n-1}+\cdots+a_{n-1}x_2 +a_nx_1 = 0. \]

Then

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\\\vdots\\x_{n-1}\\x_{n} \end{array}\right) =\left(\begin{array}{cccccc} 0&1&0&\ldots&0&0\\0&0&1&\ldots&0&0\\0&0&0&\ldots&0&0\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&&\vdots&\vdots\\0&0&0&\ldots&0&1\\-a_n&-a_{n-1}&-a_{n-2} &\ldots&-a_2&-a_1\end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\\\vdots\\x_{n-1}\\x_{n}\end{array}\right). \end{split}\]

Example 1.1.2 Consider a situation in which three thermally insulated tanks each containing an initial volume \(V\) of liquid are connected so that the liquid flows between the tanks at rates as shown and there is a heat flow \(u(t)\) into the first tank, N.B. heat flow \(=\) liquid flow \(\times\) heat per unit volume. Then, if \(x_1\), \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) are the amounts of heat in tanks \(1, 2\) and \(3\) respectively

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}x_1}{\mathrm{d}t} &= u(t)+\frac{2Rx_3}{V} -\frac{2Rx_1}{V},\\ \frac{\mathrm{d}x_2}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{Rx_1}{V}-\frac{Rx_2}{V},\\ \frac{\mathrm{d}x_3}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{Rx_1}{V}+\frac{Rx_2}{V}-\frac{2Rx_3}{V}. \end{split}\]

That is

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{array}\right) = \frac{R}{V}\left(\begin{array}{ccc}-2&0&2\\1&-1&0\\1&1&-2\end{array}\right)\left (\begin{array}{c}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{array}\right) + \left(\begin{array}{c}u(t)\\0\\0\end{array}\right), \end{split}\]

or

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}}{\mathrm{d}t} = A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{f}(t), \]

where

\[\begin{split} \mathbf{x}= \begin{pmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{pmatrix},\quad A=\frac{R}{V}\left(\begin{array}{rrr}-2&0&2\\1&-1&0\\1&1&-2\end{array}\right) \text{ and }\mathbf{f}(t) = \begin{pmatrix} u(t)\\ 0\\ 0\end{pmatrix}, \end{split}\]

represents a forcing or control effect on the system.

Now, suppose that, instead of the flow rates being adjusted so that the volume in each of the tanks remains constant, the flow rate from tank \(1\) to tank \(3\) is \(R+r\). Then, the volume of liquid in tank \(1\) at time \(t\) is \(V-rt\) and the volume in tank \(3\) is \(V+rt\). It follows that

\[\begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}x_1}{\mathrm{d}t} &= u(t)+\frac{2Rx_3}{V+rt} -\frac{(2R+r)}{V-rt}x_1,\\ \frac{\mathrm{d}x_2}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{Rx_1}{V-rt}-\frac{Rx_2}{V},\\ \frac{\mathrm{d}x_3}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{(R+r)}{V-rt}x_1+\frac{Rx_2}{V}-\frac{2Rx_3}{V+rt}, \end{split}\]

or

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}}{\mathrm{d}t} = A(t)\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{f}(t), \]

where

\[\begin{split} A=\left(\begin{array}{rrc}-\frac{2R+r}{V-rt}&0&\frac{2R}{V+rt}\\\frac{R}{V-rt} &-\frac{R}{V}&0\\\frac{R+r}{V-rt}&\frac{R}{V}&-\frac{2R}{V+rt}\end{array}\right). \end{split}\]

In this case the coefficients in the system of differential equations are not constant; however, these differential equations are still linear.


Example 1.1.3 Consider a situation in which liquid of density \(\rho\) is pumped into a tank at rate \(w_I\) and then flows into another tank through a valve at rate \(w\). If \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) are the heights of liquids in tanks \(1\) and \(2\) respectively, then the mass continuity equations for the tanks are

\[\begin{split} \rho A_1\frac{\mathrm{d}h_1}{\mathrm{d}t} &= w_I-w = w_I-c\sqrt{\rho(h_1-h_2)},\\ \rho A_2\frac{\mathrm{d}h_2}{\mathrm{d}t} &= w = c\sqrt{\rho(h_1-h_2)}. \end{split}\]

In this case a non-linear system of equations has been obtained. Non-linear systems cannot be solved by the analytical techniques considered in this course rather numerical techniques are typically considered (See PDEs and/or NADS modules). It may also be possible to approximate a non-linear system by a linear one in some circumstances.


Example 1.1.4 Suppose that an animal population can be classified in three ways: young \((1)\), mature \((1-2)\) and old \((2+)\); and that survival rates and birth rates from the three categories are as shown.

Then if \(y_k, m_k\) and \(d_k\) are the number in each of the three categories at the start of the \(k\)th year, it follows that

\[\begin{split} y_{k+1} &= b_1m_k+b_2d_k,\\ m_{k+1} &= s_1y_k,\\ d_{k+1} &= s_2m_k+s_3d_k. \end{split}\]

That is,

\[ \mathbf{p}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{p}_k, \]

where

\[\begin{split} \mathbf{p}_k=\begin{pmatrix} y_k\\m_k\\d_k\end{pmatrix} \text{ and } A = \left(\begin{array}{ccc}0&b_1&b_2\\s_1&0&0\\0&s_2&s_3\end{array}\right). \end{split}\]
